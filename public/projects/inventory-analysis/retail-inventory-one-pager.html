01 - Objectives

What this project
set out to answer

// Objective 01

Diagnose Inventory System Failures

Identify which products are experiencing stockouts and overstock - not as a prediction exercise, but as a diagnosis of where the system is failing today.

// Objective 02

Quantify the Financial Consequence

Translate inventory failures into business terms: how much revenue is lost to empty shelves, how much cash is locked in excess stock, and where the exposure is highest.

// Objective 03

Surface Actionable Reorder Recommendations

Produce immediately actionable reorder point corrections using actual sales data - without requiring a single machine learning model.

// Objective 04

Build Production-Grade Infrastructure

Go beyond a notebook. Implement a fully layered warehouse with RAW, CORE, and MART separation, automated testing, and documented models.

// Objective 05

Validate Forecast Accuracy

Assess whether the existing demand forecast is calibrated correctly, and identify directional bias that compounds inventory errors over time.

// Objective 06

Rank Stores by Operational Performance

Score stores across five metrics and produce a ranked leaderboard to guide where targeted intervention would have the highest impact.

02 - Problem Statement

Two costly errors.
One shared root cause.

// Too Little Stock

Stockout

01Lost sales when the shelf is empty and the customer shops elsewhere.
02Repeat customers switch to a competitor when they can't get their order.
03Emergency replenishment at inflated cost and expedited shipping premiums.
// Too Much Stock

Overstock

01Cash tied up in products sitting unsold on shelves.
02Markdowns and discounts required to clear excess inventory.
03Perishable or seasonal goods written off entirely.
04Storage costs accumulate on slow-moving lines.
⚡

Both errors share the same root cause: reorder points and demand forecasts that don't reflect actual sales patterns. Order too late and you run out. Over-correct and you carry too much. The fix is not more stock - it's better configuration.

04 - Key Findings

What the data
actually showed

// Finding 01 · Critical

Reorder Points Are Critically Underset Across Every Product

Every product in the catalogue has a reorder point set far below what actual sales data requires. The worst case - Casual T-Shirt - is configured to trigger at 113 units when the data-driven correct threshold is 1,943 units. A gap of 1,830 units. By the time a reorder fires, there is not enough stock to last through the supplier's delivery window. Stockouts are structurally inevitable under the current configuration.

// Finding 02

Demand Forecasts Systematically Overestimate Sales

Across all 20 products, forecasts consistently overestimate actual sales by ~3.5%. A one-directional bias across the entire catalogue signals the forecast model needs recalibration - it compounds the overstock problem over time.

3.5%

// Finding 03 · Counterintuitive

Products Are Simultaneously Overstocked and Stockingout

Canned Tomatoes is overstocked on 50% of trading days yet still experiences stockouts. Stock arrives in large batches, sells down, runs out before the next order, and the cycle repeats. The problem is timing, not volume.

// Finding 04

Inventory Turnover Is Below Benchmark Across the Catalogue

Healthy retail targets 4–12x turnover per year. This dataset ranges 0.72–1.25x. Action Figure Set and Classic LEGO Set at 0.72 tie up the most cash relative to velocity.

05 - Recommendations

What to do about it

1
Recalculate Reorder Points Immediately
Every product's reorder point should be recalculated using avg daily sales × supplier lead time. Casual T-Shirt, Organic Oats, and USB-C Hub carry the largest gaps. Until this is corrected, stockouts are structurally unavoidable regardless of order volume.
High Priority
2
Recalibrate the Demand Forecast Model
A consistent 3.5% overestimate across every product is a systematic bias, not random error. Adjusting the model's inputs to reduce this directional skew will improve order accuracy and reduce the $20.7M currently tied up in excess stock.
High Priority
3
Shift High Overstock/Stockout Products to Smaller, Frequent Orders
For Canned Tomatoes, Smart Home Speaker, and Dining Table Oak, the solution is not ordering more but ordering more frequently in smaller batches. The replenishment cycle is the problem - smoother deliveries eliminate the boom-and-bust pattern driving both metrics wrong simultaneously.
Medium Priority
4
Activate Sleeping Giants with Targeted Promotions
Classic LEGO Set and Building Blocks 100pc are high-margin products sitting in the Low Velocity quadrant. Targeted promotional intervention - discounting or bundling - could unlock strong profit without adding inventory risk.
Strategic
5
Prioritise Intervention at S005 and S004
Rather than applying catalogue-wide fixes uniformly, operational effort should be concentrated at the lowest-performing stores where financial exposure is highest. Use S002 operating patterns as the internal benchmark.
Medium Priority
6
Review Eight Products With Turnover Below 0.85×
Action Figure Set and Classic LEGO Set tie up the most cash relative to sales velocity. Discounting or bundling strategies would accelerate sell-through and improve overall capital efficiency across the catalogue.
Strategic
